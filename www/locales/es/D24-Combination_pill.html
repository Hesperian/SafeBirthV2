Combination pills include both the hormones estrogen and progestin, similar to the hormones produced in your body. Different brands contain different amounts of each hormone. Some brands change the amounts of either hormone in each pill once or twice during the month—you can usually tell because the color of the pill changes. Different amounts of hormones cause different side effects, so you may find that some brands of combination pills work better for you than others.

  • How to use combination pills

    If you are sure you aren't pregnant, you can start taking the pill any time. Pills will not prevent pregnancy until you have been taking them for about a week. So during the first 7 days after starting birth control pills, use condoms or avoid sex.

    You must take 1 pill every day to prevent pregnancy, even if you don't have sex that day. Try to take your pill at the same time each day. If you keep the pills where you sleep, it may help you remember to take one each night before bed or first thing when you get up in the morning.

    Most combination pills come in packets of 28 or 21 pills.

    28-day pack: Take the pills with hormones for 21 days, then for 7 days take the reminder pills (the extra pills in the packet have no hormones) or take no pills. You will have bleeding each month during those 7 days like a normal period.

    21-day pack: Take 1 pill every day for 21 days—the whole packet. Then don't take a pill for 7 days. Then start a new packet.

    If you forget to take a pill:

    If you forget to take 1 or 2 pills, take 1 pill as soon as you remember. Then take the next pill at the regular time. This may mean that you take 2 pills in one day.

    If you forget to take 3 pills, 3 days in a row, take 1 pill right away. Then take 1 pill each day at the regular time. Use condoms or don't have sex until you start your period or until you have taken a pill for 7 days in a row.

  • Many women think combination pills are great because…
    • When you take them every day, you are protected without having to do anything else.
    • No one can tell you are using birth control but you.
    • You have a regular period.
    • If you want to get pregnant, you can as soon as you stop taking them.
    But some women don’t like combination pills because:
    • They can’t remember to take them every day.
    • They don’t want to use hormones.
    • They don’t want to have irregular bleeding or spotting.
    • They don’t want to gain weight.
    • Sometimes they can cause headaches, nausea, tender breasts, or other discomforts.
  • Who shouldn’t use it

    Don’t use combination pills if you: are breastfeeding a child under 6 months old; are over 35-years old and smoke tobacco, or have migraine headaches with an aura; have liver problems (jaundice, cirrhosis, tumors); ever had breast cancer; have had diabetes for 20 years or physical damage from diabetes; have gallbladder disease; have high blood pressure or have had a stroke, or blood clots in your lungs or legs. Use a method without hormones instead.

    Combination pills don't work as well when they interact with certain medicines. Don't use them if you are taking medicines for seizures, rifampicin (for TB), or ritonavir (for HIV).
