The vaginal ring is a small plastic ring you put into your vagina. It slowly releases estrogen and progestin. Protection lasts for 1 month, then you replace it.

  • How to use

    With clean hands, squeeze the ring and gently insert it deep into your vagina. The ring is placed correctly if you can't feel it when you walk or move around. The ring can be left in the vagina during sex. Or you can remove it, but it must be put back in within 3 hours to continue giving protection. (If it is out for more than 3 hours, use a condom or avoid sex for 7 days.)

    After 3 weeks, remove the ring by putting your finger into your vagina and pulling it out. Menstruation will happen during week 4. Then insert a new one.

    If you don't want to menstruate, it is safe to leave the ring in for 4 weeks and then replace it right away. You may have a little spotting at first, but the ring remains effective against pregnancy and your body remains healthy.

  • Many women think the vaginal ring is great because…
    • When you put in the vaginal ring once every 4 weeks, you are protected without having to do anything else.
    • Occasionally a partner might feel the ring, but you can take it out for up to 3 hours if that concerns you. So no one can tell you are using birth control but you.
    • You have a regular period if you remove it after 3 weeks; or you can have no period if you keep it in for 4 weeks.
    • If you want to get pregnant, you can as soon as you take it out.
    • It contains less hormones than other methods, so has fewer side effects.
    But some women don’t like the vaginal ring because:
    • They don’t want to use hormones.
    • They don’t want to have to put anything in their vagina.
    • They don’t want to have irregular bleeding or spotting.
    • Sometimes it can cause headaches, nausea, tender breasts, or other discomforts.
    • If you are storing it for more than 4 months, it needs to be refrigerated.
  • Who shouldn’t use it

    Don’t use the vaginal ring if you: are breastfeeding a child under 6-months old; are over 35-years old and smoke tobacco, or have migraine headaches with an aura; have liver problems (jaundice, cirrhosis, tumors); ever had breast cancer; have had diabetes for 20 years or physical damage from diabetes; have gallbladder disease; have high blood pressure or have had a stroke.

    The vaginal ring doesn’t work as well when it interacts with certain medicines. Don’t use it if you are taking medicines for seizures, rifampicin (for TB), or ritonavir (for HIV).
