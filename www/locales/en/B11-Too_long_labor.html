The hormonal IUD (Mirena, Kyleena, Skyla and others) is a small piece of plastic placed in the uterus. It releases a small amount of the hormone progestin to prevent pregnancy. Hormonal IUDs can last between 3 and 5 years, depending on the brand. They often make menstrual flow lighter.

  • How to use

    The best time to have the IUD put in is during menstruation, when you are certain you aren't pregnant. An IUD must be inserted and removed by a specially trained health worker. After childbirth, it is best to wait 6 weeks for the womb to return to its normal size and shape before getting an IUD.

    Check the IUD strings after each menstruation to make sure it is in place. Here’s how: wash your hands, squat, and reach as far as you can into your vagina with 2 fingers. Feel for the IUD strings, but don't pull them. If you can feel them, the IUD is in the correct place. If you cannot, the IUD may have slipped out of place and will no longer prevent pregnancy—see a health worker.

  • Many women think hormonal IUDs are great because…
    • After it is put in, you are protected without having to do anything but check once a month that it is still there.
    • Sometimes a partner might feel its strings, but mostly no one can tell you are using birth control but you.
    • It lasts for 3 to 5 years, depending on the type.
    • Your menstruation is lighter, and might even stop.
    • If you want to get pregnant, you can as soon as you get the IUD removed.
    But some women don’t like hormonal IUDs because:
    • They don’t want to use a hormonal method.
    • They don’t want their period to stop.
    • They don’t want to have irregular bleeding or spotting.
  • Who shouldn’t use it

    Don't use a hormonal IUD if you: have liver problems (jaundice, cirrhosis, tumors); ever had breast cancer; have blood clots in your lungs or legs. Use a method without hormones instead.

    IUDs are not a good choice if you: are likely to have gonorrhea or chlamydia; have AIDS or uncontrolled HIV; have unexplained vaginal bleeding; have genital cancer or pelvic tuberculosis.
