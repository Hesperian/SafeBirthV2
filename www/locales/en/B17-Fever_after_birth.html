The patch is a thin piece of plastic that sticks to the skin and releases the hormones estrogen and progestin into your body. Protection from each patch lasts for 1 week, then you replace it.

  • How to use

    Place the patch on clean, dry skin anywhere on your body, except your breasts. If you put it on during the first 5 days of your period, it works right away. If not, use a condom during sex for the first week of the patch. Replace the patch with a new one the same day each week for 3 weeks in a row, then use no patch for 1 week (the week of menstruation).

  • Many women think the patch is great because…
    • When you put on a patch once a week, you are protected without having to do anything else.
    • You have a regular period.
    • If you want to get pregnant, you can as soon as you take it off.
    But some women don’t like the patch because:
    • They don’t want to use hormones.
    • They don’t want to have irregular bleeding or spotting.
    • Sometimes it can cause headaches, nausea, tender breasts, or other discomforts.
    • Sometimes it irritates the skin under the patch.
    • It only comes in one skin color: beige.
  • Who shouldn’t use it

    Don’t use the patch if you: are breastfeeding a child under 6 months old; are over 35-years old and smoke tobacco, or have migraine headaches with an aura; have liver problems (jaundice, cirrhosis, tumors); ever had breast cancer; have had diabetes for 20 years or physical damage from diabetes; have gallbladder disease; have high blood pressure or have had a stroke, or blood clots in your lungs or legs. Use a method without hormones instead.

    The patch doesn’t work as well when it interacts with certain medicines. Don’t use it if you are taking medicines for seizures, rifampicin (for TB), or ritonavir (for HIV).
