Because women’s bodies can get pregnant, the right to contraception—the right to decide when you do and do not want to get pregnant—is central to women’s right to health.

Women are healthier when they can decide for themselves when to have sex, and if and when to have children. Access to contraception enables women to make those decisions.

  • I think I’m pregnant and I don't want to be. What should I do?

    Don’t panic. First, if you had unprotected sex within 5 days, you can use emergency pills to prevent getting pregnant. If your period is late, you can get an inexpensive test that will tell you if you are pregnant. If you are pregnant but don't want to be, you can interrupt your pregnancy with a safe abortion (see Hesperian’s Safe Abortion app). What is important is to move quickly—every option available to you is simpler and works better the sooner you use it.

Birth control overview

  • What is family planning?

    Family planning is having the number of children you want, when you want them. This app explains the many safe, effective methods to prevent pregnancy also called birth control or contraception. You can usually get them from health workers, clinics or pharmacies, sometimes for free or at low cost.

  • What is contraception?

    Contraception means preventing pregnancy. This app explains the many safe, effective methods to prevent pregnancy also called birth control or family planning. You can usually get them from health workers, clinics or pharmacies, sometimes for free or at low cost.

  • Why is birth control important?
    • Deciding if and when you want to have children, without others telling you that you should or should not, gives you more control of your life.
    • Enjoying sex without worrying about getting pregnant is better for your emotional and physical health.
    • Contraception helps you avoid unsafe abortion, which kills many thousands of women every year.
  • I am married. Why is family planning important for me?
    • Just because you are married doesn’t mean you have finished your education, don't have employment goals or other things you wish to accomplish before having children.
    • Deciding if and when you want to have children, without others telling you that you should or should not, gives you more control of your life.
    • Waiting to have children and taking time between children can allow you to make a secure life for them (and yourself), and gives you more time, energy, and money to care for the children you already have
    • Having fewer children is healthier for a woman’s body than having many. By using family planning, you can decide when your body is healthy enough to be pregnant.
    • Sex is more enjoyable without worrying about getting pregnant if you or your partner do not want or are not ready to have a child.
    • Family planning helps women avoid unsafe abortion, which kills many thousands of women every year.
  • I’m not married, but I need birth control. Why is it hard for me to get?

    Some people think birth control is only for married women, that single women shouldn't have sex. But both married and unmarried people have sex, and many women want to be able to enjoy sex without worrying about getting pregnant. Also, women don't always have a choice about having sex. Some are pressured, and some are forced. Without family planning, any woman, married or unmarried, young or older, can get pregnant. Anyone who wants to prevent pregnancy can benefit from family planning. If you are a health worker, it is important that you share what you know about family planning with all women. Helping men understand the benefits of family planning can help them overcome their fears about it and understand how family planning helps them too.

Birth control pills

  • I hear birth control pills cause cancer. Is that true?

    No. Birth control pills don't cause cancer and combined pills may even protect you against getting cancer of the ovaries and cancer of the lining of the uterus (endometrial cancer).

  • The drugstore no longer sells the brand of pills I have been using. What should I do?

    Look on your package of pills to find out how much estrogen, or estrogen and progesterone, your brand contains. Show it to the pharmacist or your health care provider and ask them what other brands of pills contain the same quantity of hormones. Usually you can find something that is the same or very similar.

  • I think using birth control pills have made my vagina dryer so sex is less enjoyable. What should I do?

    To increase how wet your vagina gets, you can try several things. First, have your partner slow down his love-making and give you time to get aroused. Second, try using a lubricant. There are many kinds available, and you can find one that works with your birth control. Don’t use an oil-based lubricant with latex condoms and don’t use silicone-based lubricants with diaphragms or cervical caps. Water-based lubricants can be used with any method. Finally, you can see if a pill that contains more estrogen helps with your wetness and doesn’t cause any other problem side effects. Or you can change to a non-hormonal method completely.

  • Taking birth control pills makes my breasts hurt and gives me a headache. What should I do?

    These problems often go away after using pills for a few months. If these problems continue, you can ask your pharmacist or health care provider to have you try a combination pill with less estrogen, or a progestin-only pill. If that doesn’t help, then consider switching to a non-hormonal method.

  • After I started taking pills, my period has almost stopped. Is this normal?

    Both combined pills and progestin-only mini pills will reduce the length of your periods and lighten them, sometimes until they stop. This is very common and not harmful.

  • My friend starts a new packet of birth control pills after 3 weeks (instead of 4) and no longer has a period. Is this healthy?

    Yes. Using pills so that you have no period creates absolutely no problems for your body.

  • How can I tell if the pills I buy are real and not fake?

    Get your pills from a trustworthy source. Look on the internet to find pictures of what the pills and packaging is supposed to look like. Make sure the package is sealed and the pills are the right size and color. A more common problem is getting pills that are too old to work well. Check the expiration date on the packaging.

IUDs

  • I hear IUDs can make it so you can never have children. Is this true?

    Not any more. Many years ago, there was an IUD called the Dalkon Shield that led to infections causing sterility in some women but it was taken off the market. All IUDs are now safe to use. After you have an IUD removed, you can get pregnant right away.

  • I worry that my IUD will fall out. Can that happen?

    In very rare instances, an IUD will slip or move inside you. That’s why you should check the strings attached to the IUD once a month. If you discover it has moved, see your health provider so it can be better placed or removed. And use another method of contraception until it is, because you might be able to get pregnant.

  • My period got really heavy when I started using an IUD. They told me to wait a while, it would lighten up, but it hasn’t. What should I do?

    That kind of increased bleeding happens with copper IUDs. You could try a hormonal IUD instead which reduces bleeding, or try a different method.

Emergency pills

  • I don’t have sex very often. Is it safe to take emergency pills whenever I have sex to keep from getting pregnant?

    Emergency contraception was designed to work in an emergency. It contains more hormones than daily use birth control pills, so it has more side effects. It is also much more expensive than regular birth control pills. While using them as a regular method won't cause illness, it isn't the healthiest way to prevent pregnancy. If sex is now a regular or even semi-regular part of your life, you will enjoy it more if you start a regular method that lets you control when or if you will get pregnant.

  • I’ve heard that emergency pills are bad for you and make it harder to have children. Is this true?

    No. They only prevent you from getting pregnant when you use them correctly. They don’t harm you at all, and unless you start a method of birth control, you can get pregnant again right away.

  • What happens if I use emergency pills more than 5 days after having unprotected sex?

    Emergency contraception only stops pregnancy within 5 days of having sex. If you are pregnant, after 5 days you will continue to be pregnant and the pills won't harm the pregnancy or cause birth defects.

  • How can I know if the emergency pills have worked?

    You will know the emergency pills worked when you get your period within a week of when it would normally come. Or you can wait 3 weeks and take a pregnancy test to be sure.

  • I’ve heard that using emergency pills is the same as getting an abortion. Is this true?

    No. Emergency pills work like other birth control pills—they prevent pregnancy from happening.

Barriers

  • What should I do if a condom comes off inside me or breaks?

    If you have a condom disaster when you aren't in your fertile days, then you don’t have to worry about pregnancy (but you do need to worry about STIs). If you are not sure where you are in your cycle, the safest thing to do to prevent pregnancy is to take emergency pills. That way you’ll know you won't get pregnant.

  • What if the condom doesn’t fit well?

    While sex education classes love to have you put a condom on a banana, the fact is that not all condoms fit everyone. If a condom comes off during sex, or feels like it might, the problem isn’t with you or your guy—it’s with the condom. There are different sizes and kinds of condoms and they are all fairly inexpensive, so try different brands if you are having problems with a loose condom. When condoms are too tight or just don’t fit well, they can hurt or distract the man, make sex less enjoyable, and cause his penis to get less hard—which might make the condom come off! And for about ⅓ of all men, a smaller size condom fits better than the standard size. Try different sizes, styles and brands. Finding a condom that makes you and your partner happy is also a good way to stop him from complaining about using one. If a condom comes off during sex, you can still protect yourself, see emergency pills.

  • I have a condom that has been in my purse for a year. Is it still safe to use?

    First check the expiration date printed on the package. If it is past the expiration date, don't use it. If it is ok, check the package by pushing on it a little to see if there is a cushion of air inside, showing that it is still sealed. If it isn't sealed, don’t use it. Condoms keep best in cool dark places.

  • My partner doesn’t want to use a condom because he says it makes sex less pleasurable. How can I change his mind?

    It helps to be clear before you start that the only way you will have sex is if he wears a condom. Communication is important in a relationship, even a very brief one. You can say that sex won't be pleasurable for you if you have to worry about getting pregnant or getting an STI. Promise that the sex will be amazing for both of you, and the condom will let both of you focus on each other instead of worrying. And if you have some condoms available, you won’t have to depend on him bringing them.

  • Can I reuse a condom if I clean it carefully?

    No.

  • Can spermicide harm you if it gets in the mouth?

    It might not taste good, but it isn't dangerous. Remember, the vagina is one of the most absorbent places in a woman’s body, and if spermicide doesn’t hurt there it won’t hurt in the mouth either.

  • What happens if the cervical cap or diaphragm slips or changes position during sex?

    While this can happen, it isn't common. If you can feel it out of place, then you can re-adjust before continuing, but in the middle of sex it might not be possible. The spermicide you use with a diaphragm or cervical cap lessens the possibility that this kind of accident will lead to pregnancy, but do the same thing as when a condom breaks to be sure. Putting in a diaphragm or a cervical cap properly takes practice, so do it several times before using it for real.

Patch

  • Can I wear the patch while bathing or swimming? Playing sports or working hard when I sweat a lot?

    The patch is pretty sticky and usually stays on during all activities.

  • After I first put the patch on, can I move it to a different place on my body?

    It is best not to, but if you must you can if it continues to stick on your skin. The hormones for the patch are mixed together with the adhesive, so if it doesn’t stick well by itself, it won’t be working. In other words, don’t try taping it on—it won’t work. If it doesn't stick on securely anymore, put on a new patch instead.

  • Can I take the patch off before having sex and then put it back on afterwards?

    It is best not to, but if you must you can so long as you put it back on within 24 hours AND it continues to stick on your skin. The hormones for the patch are mixed together with the adhesive, so if it doesn’t stick well by itself, it won’t be working. In other words, don’t try taping it on — it won’t work. Put on a new patch instead.

General

  • My women’s clinic doesn't give the kind of birth control I want to use. What should I do?

    Talk to the health workers at the clinic and give them information about the method and why you and other women want to use it. Maybe if they know demand for it is high they will begin to carry it. If the reason they don’t carry it is religious, complain to the authorities about how they are limiting women’s choices unfairly. Look for alternative sources of contraception.

  • I have been using birth control, but now I want to get pregnant. How long do I have to wait before I can get pregnant?

    While it is different for different methods and different women, for most methods you can get pregnant right after you stop using them. Injections may take a little longer, and implants the longest.

  • What’s the difference between family planning, birth control, and contraception?

    They all mean the same thing: that you can enjoy sex without worrying about getting pregnant until you want to.
